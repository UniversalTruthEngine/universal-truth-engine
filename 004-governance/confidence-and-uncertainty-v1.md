# Confidence and Uncertainty Standard v1

## Purpose

This document defines how UTE represents confidence, uncertainty, and validity boundaries.

UTE should avoid false equality between claims of different epistemic strength.

Mathematical proof, reproducible experiment, strong scientific consensus, and provisional modelling should not appear identical.

---

## 1. Confidence Levels

### Level 5 — Mathematical / Logical Proof

Universally derivable within a defined formal system.

Examples:
- arithmetic identities,
- Pythagorean theorem in Euclidean geometry,
- law of identity.

### Level 4 — Reproducible Physical Law

Confirmed repeatedly through independent observation or experiment within known conditions.

Examples:
- conservation laws,
- Ohm’s law within applicable regimes,
- basic thermodynamic principles.

### Level 3 — Strong Scientific Consensus

Supported by substantial evidence and broad expert agreement, but open to refinement.

Examples:
- plate tectonics,
- evolution by natural selection,
- germ theory.

### Level 2 — Provisional / Model-Based

Useful predictive frameworks with unresolved assumptions or limited domains.

Examples:
- economic models,
- climate projections,
- some nutritional guidance.

### Level 1 — Speculative

Interesting but insufficiently verified.

Examples:
- emerging hypotheses,
- unverified models,
- conjectural frameworks.

---

## 2. Confidence Is Not Absoluteness

A high-confidence truth may still have conditions of validity.

Example:

```text
Newtonian mechanics
```

is highly useful and strongly validated within ordinary scales, but it is not the most complete model at relativistic or quantum scales.

---

## 3. Uncertainty Must Be Explicit

Each Core Truth should state:

- what is known,
- what is assumed,
- what remains uncertain,
- where the claim fails,
- and whether the confidence level may change.

---

## 4. Mathematical vs Empirical Confidence

Mathematical truth depends on definitions, axioms, and proof.

Empirical truth depends on observation, experiment, measurement, and reproducibility.

These should not be confused.

---

## 5. Confidence Drift

Confidence may change over time.

Changes should be recorded in review history.

---

## 6. Visual Representation

In the UTE map, confidence may eventually be shown through:

- colour,
- brightness,
- stability indicator,
- or uncertainty halo.

---

## 7. Final Principle

UTE should preserve confidence gradients.

A useful truth system must show not only what is claimed, but how strongly and under what conditions it is known.
